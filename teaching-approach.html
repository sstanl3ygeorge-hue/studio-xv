How I Approach Teaching Music Production

Learning production shouldn't feel like drowning in information. Here's how I think about teaching, and why context matters more than content.

There are thousands of production tutorials online. Most of them are technically accurate. Some are even well-made. But if you've spent months watching videos and still can't finish tracks that sound professional, the problem isn't what you're learning—it's how you're learning it.

Tutorials work when you already know enough to apply the information. They're reference material, not instruction. What they don't give you is context: why this technique instead of another, when to use it, how it fits into your specific workflow, and what to do when it doesn't work the way it's supposed to.

The Information Overload Problem

Production software gives you access to hundreds of plugins, thousands of presets, and infinite routing options. It's overwhelming by design. Without a framework for making decisions, you end up second-guessing every choice, tweaking endlessly, and never finishing anything.

Most tutorials make this worse. They show you what buttons to press without explaining why you'd press them. So you copy the steps, but when your track sounds different—which it will, because every project is different—you don't know how to troubleshoot.

What you need isn't more techniques. It's the ability to diagnose problems, make confident decisions, and understand the reasoning behind those decisions. That's what improves with practice, and it's what tutorials can't teach you.

Learning in Context

This is why I structure lessons around your own music. Not example projects, not demonstration files—your actual tracks.

When we work together, we're not learning compression in the abstract. We're fixing the vocal that's sitting too far back in your mix. We're not memorizing EQ frequency charts. We're carving out space so your kick and bass don't fight each other in your specific arrangement.

The context gives you something tutorials can't: immediate feedback on whether what you're doing is working. You hear the difference in real time. You understand why one approach works better than another. And you build intuition by solving real problems, not hypothetical ones.

Pace and Confidence

Production skills don't develop linearly. Some things click immediately. Others take weeks to internalize. Tutorials assume everyone learns at the same speed, which is why they either move too fast or feel painfully slow.

One-to-one lessons adjust to where you're at. If something makes sense quickly, we move on. If it doesn't, we break it down until it does. There's no pressure to keep up with a pre-recorded video, and no awkwardness about asking questions.

Confidence comes from repetition and small wins. Finishing a mix that sounds good. Understanding why your track translates well on different systems. Knowing how to fix a problem without googling it. Those moments accumulate, and eventually, you stop questioning every decision.

Who This Approach Works For

This isn't for everyone. If you're looking for a quick fix or a magic plugin recommendation, you'll be disappointed. If you want someone to mix your tracks for you, that's a different service entirely.

This is for producers who want to understand what they're doing and why. People who are tired of copying techniques without results. People who have specific problems they can't solve and want direct, actionable guidance.

It's also for anyone who learns better by doing than by watching. If you've ever felt like you understand something in theory but can't apply it in practice, working through your own projects is the fastest way to close that gap.

What to Expect

Sessions are conversational. We start with where you're stuck, what you're trying to achieve, or what you want to improve. Then we work through it together—screen sharing, troubleshooting, explaining concepts as they come up.

There's no fixed curriculum because everyone's starting point is different. Some people need help with arrangement. Others are solid on structure but struggle with mixing. Some just want feedback on whether their tracks are ready for release.

What's consistent is the focus on building skills that compound. Understanding gain staging makes mixing easier. Understanding compression makes mastering easier. Each session builds on the last, even if we're working on completely different aspects of production.

Why This Matters

You don't need more information. You need better application. The goal isn't to memorize techniques—it's to develop the judgment to know which technique to use, when to use it, and how to adapt it to your specific situation.

That's what separates producers who finish tracks from producers who endlessly tweak without results. And it's what one-to-one teaching is built for.
